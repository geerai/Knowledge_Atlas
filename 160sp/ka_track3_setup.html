Track 3 Setup — AI & VR Room Transformation

Install Blender, sign up for a text-to-3D service, and download your first benchmark room models. This setup gets you working in Week 1. The Unreal Engine pipeline is a future addition for the VR sub-team — it is not needed to start.

Prerequisites

  • Windows 10/11, macOS 13+, or Linux.
  • 8 GB RAM minimum; 16 GB recommended for Blender with textured scenes.
  • A GPU helps but is not strictly required. Blender's Eevee renderer works on most modern integrated graphics. If you have a discrete GPU (RTX 2060 / M1 Pro / equivalent), Cycles rendering and later Unreal work will be significantly faster.
  • 10 GB free disk for Blender, downloaded room models, and generated assets.
  • Python 3.11+, Git as in the other tracks.
  • Blender 4.x (free) — the primary tool for this track. Download from blender.org. Choose the LTS build if you prefer stability over the newest features.
  • A text-to-3D account — either Meshy or Tripo3D. Both services take a prompt (or an image) and return a textured mesh in a few minutes. Free tiers are enough for classwork; pick one and stay with it so your asset provenance is consistent.

Step-by-Step

1Install Blender 4.x

Download from blender.org, install, and open it. Blender is the neutral environment where you will inspect, edit, and export room models throughout the quarter. If you have never used Blender, spend 20 minutes on the official "Blender Fundamentals" playlist on YouTube — you need to know how to navigate the viewport, select objects, and open the properties panel.

2Download 3–4 benchmark room models

Your first task in Week 1 is to assemble a benchmark corpus of room models that the class will use for the rest of the quarter. You need real 3D rooms — not floor plans, not images, not generated text descriptions. Download at least 3 candidate rooms in .glb, .fbx, or .obj format from the sources below.

Sketchfab

Largest free model library. Filter by "downloadable" and "room" or "interior." Many models come with textures and materials. Export as .glb.

TurboSquid

Professional-grade models. Free tier has enough for classwork. Look for rooms with clean mesh hierarchy (named objects, not one merged blob).

Poly Haven

CC0-licensed HDRIs, textures, and some interior models. Excellent for lighting and material reference even if you source rooms elsewhere.

AI-generated rooms

Use Meshy or Tripo3D to generate a room from a prompt like "a simple residential living room, 3m × 4m, one window, one door, wooden floor." The result will need significant cleanup but is a valid starting point.

What makes a good benchmark room. You want rooms where the mesh hierarchy is inspectable (walls, floor, ceiling, windows, doors are separate objects or clearly named), the scale is realistic (a door is ~2m tall, not 20m), and there are enough architectural features to support the six core transformation factors (ceiling height, wall geometry, prospect/refuge, light, materials, texture). A beautiful render of a room that is one fused mesh is useless for this track.

3Import a room into Blender and inspect it

Open Blender, go to File → Import, and choose the format matching your download (.glb, .fbx, or .obj). Then inspect:

  • Scale: Is a door roughly 2m tall? If the room is 200 units tall, you need to rescale. In Blender's sidebar (press N), check dimensions.
  • Hierarchy: In the Outliner panel (top-right), expand the scene tree. Are walls, floor, ceiling, and openings separate objects? Or is everything one merged mesh?
  • Materials: Select an object and check the Material Properties tab. Does it have named materials you can swap or adjust?
  • Openings: Are windows and doors actual holes in the wall geometry, or just texture decals painted on a flat surface?

This inspection is the basis of your Week 1 "room sheet" deliverable.

4Set up your text-to-3D pipeline

Sign up for Meshy or Tripo3D. Generate one test object — try "a round wooden side-table, 45 cm tall, mid-century style" — and download the result as .glb.

Import it into Blender and check:

  • Is the scale correct? (The table should be ~0.45m, not 45m.)
  • Are there stray vertices or disconnected geometry?
  • How many materials were generated? Can you consolidate them?
Why Blender sits in the middle. Meshes from text-to-3D services are rarely clean enough to use directly — scales are off by a factor of ten, UV seams are visible, and materials are sometimes duplicated. Blender is the neutral editor where you correct these issues once. Skipping cleanup is the most common reason a generated prop looks wrong later.

  • benchmark_rooms/ — downloaded room models and their room sheets (Week 1)
  • generated_assets/ — props from Meshy/Tripo3D after Blender cleanup
  • variants/ — transformed versions of benchmark rooms
  • logs/ — transformation logs, validation checklists, metadata

Common Snags

  • Blender viewport is black or grey on open — press Numpad . to focus on the selected object, or Home to frame everything. The camera may be pointed at empty space.
  • Imported model is invisible or tiny — the model may be at a vastly different scale. Select all (A), then S to scale, and check the dimensions in the sidebar (N).
  • FBX import has no textures — textures are often in a separate folder alongside the .fbx. Re-import with "Find Missing Files" or manually assign them in the Shader Editor.
  • Text-to-3D mesh has thousands of tiny faces — use Blender's Decimate modifier to reduce polygon count before working with it. Target 50–80% reduction as a starting point.
  • Scale is wrong (door is 20m tall) — select all objects, set the scale factor so a door is ~2.1m. Apply with Ctrl+A → Scale.
