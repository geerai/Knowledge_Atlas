## Panel Testimony

### Yaara Yeshurun: The Olfactory Pathway and Its Unique Architecture

The olfactory system is neurobiologically singular among the senses. Unlike vision, hearing, and touch—which relay through the thalamus—odorant information travels directly from the olfactory bulb to the piriform cortex, amygdala, and hippocampus. This anatomical fact is not a technicality; it is the foundation of why olfaction shapes memory, emotion, and spatial experience differently from every other sense.

To be precise about the pathway: olfactory receptor neurons in the nasal epithelium send axons through the cribriform plate to the olfactory bulb, where they synapse with mitral and tufted cells in glomeruli. Mitral cells then project directly (with no thalamic intermediary) to the piriform cortex, amygdala, and entorhinal cortex. The amygdala, in particular, is the hub that tags odors with emotional and affective significance. The hippocampus receives olfactory input both directly from piriform cortex and indirectly through amygdala and entorhinal cortex, integrating odor information into spatial and contextual memory at the earliest stages of encoding.

What CNfA has completely overlooked is that **olfaction is the only sense with direct limbic access**. This means that architectural scent is not processed as a semantic label or abstract descriptor the way a color or a sound might be. Instead, it is bound immediately to emotional and somatic state, and automatically integrated into the hippocampal spatial map. When you smell lavender in a hospital corridor, your amygdala already knows it is associated with calm before your prefrontal cortex recognizes the scent consciously. This is not a feature of higher-order semantic processing; it is a fundamental property of olfactory neuroanatomy.

Our neuroimaging work has demonstrated that odor-evoked memories activate the hippocampus and amygdala with exceptional vigor and speed. Critically, these memories are often older, more emotional, and more vividly sensory than visually cued memories. This is the "Proust phenomenon"—not merely a poetic notion, but a reproducible neurobiological effect. The direct olfactory-hippocampal-amygdala pathway creates a form of spatial memory that bypasses conscious deliberation and semantic mediation. An architectural space infused with a particular scent becomes neurologically *bound* to that space in a way that visual or acoustic cues cannot match.

A critical phenomenon architectural designers must understand is the **nasal cycle**. Olfactory processing is lateralized—at any given moment, one nostril has higher air flow and olfactory sensitivity than the other. This cycle oscillates approximately every 90 minutes to 2 hours. The lateralization of olfactory input affects which hemisphere's hippocampus receives dominant encoding signals. This may have implications for how people navigate asymmetric architectural spaces; the side of space accessible to the dominant nostril may be encoded with higher fidelity. This is unexplored in architecture.

Another critical gap: **olfactory adaptation**. The olfactory system habituates rapidly—a continuous odorant becomes imperceptible within minutes. This is not a failure of the system; it is a design principle. The olfactory system is exquisitely sensitive to *change*, not stasis. An architectural space with a static, unchanging scent will lose its olfactory character within 5-10 minutes of occupancy. However, subtle variation in concentration, or periodic introduction of novel odorants, can maintain olfactory salience indefinitely. This suggests that architectural olfaction requires dynamic scent delivery, not static diffusion.

**Proposed experiments:**
- *IV*: Nasal cycle dominance (dominant nostril vs. non-dominant); *DV*: hippocampal fMRI activation during spatial encoding of an architectural environment; *Prediction*: spaces encoded with the dominant nostril will show higher hippocampal coherence and faster memory consolidation.
- *IV*: Olfactory adaptation (0 min, 5 min, 10 min, 30 min of continuous scent exposure); *DV*: amygdala activation (fMRI) and memory integration (free recall + recognition); *Prediction*: adaptation suppresses amygdala response; brief periodic scent reintroduction maintains amygdala engagement.
- *IV*: Odor novelty in familiar space (habituated odor vs. novel odor at same location); *DV*: hippocampal theta oscillations (iEEG if available) and behavioral "place uncertainty" (reaction time to navigate); *Prediction*: novel odors reset spatial context memory, producing momentary disorientation followed by rapid re-mapping.

**Recommended specialists**: Noam Sobel (Weizmann Institute, olfactory perception and adaptation), Rachel Herz (Brown University, olfactory memory and emotion), Gordon Gallup (SUNY Albany, olfactory-limbic anatomy).

### Jay Gottfried: Olfactory Context and Spatial Memory Encoding

The piriform cortex is not merely a relay station; it is the seat of olfactory perception, learning, and—critically—contextual binding. My research has shown that the piriform cortex does not encode isolated odorants; it encodes odorants *within their contexts*. A particular smell activates different populations of neurons depending on the visual environment, the task context, and the animal's previous experience. This is computational flexibility—the olfactory system learns to treat the same molecule differently in different spatial settings.

What does this mean for architecture? It means that architectural scent is not an independent stimulus; it is neurobiologically entangled with the visual, acoustic, and spatial features of the space. The brain *learns* that a particular smell goes with a particular room, a particular activity, a particular quality of light. Over repeated visits, olfactory neurons in the piriform cortex develop a kind of "place cell" quality—they respond maximally when the animal is in the specific spatial context associated with that odor during learning.

This is a form of architectural memory I have not seen discussed in the design literature. Just as the hippocampus maintains allocentric spatial maps (world-centered representations of location), the piriform cortex seems to maintain what I would call "olfactory-spatial codes"—learned associations between odorant identity and specific locations in navigable space. The binding is bidirectional: seeing the visual features of a space reactivates olfactory representations in the piriform cortex, and smelling the odor reactivates visual and spatial representations in the hippocampus.

The neurobiological substrate of this binding is **context-dependent facilitation in piriform cortex**. When an odor is learned in a particular spatial context, repeated exposure to that context causes pyramidal neurons in piriform cortex to respond to the odor with higher gain and earlier latency. This is not simple associative learning; it is a form of "pattern completion" analogous to what we see in the hippocampus. If you place someone in the visual and acoustic environment of an architectural space without the olfactory component, the piriform cortex will partially reactivate the learned odor representation. Conversely, presenting the odor without the visual context will reactivate visual memory.

The "olfactory environment"—the ensemble of scents in a space—functions as a powerful retrieval cue for spatial memory. Consider a university library. If the library has a distinctive scent profile (leather, paper, wood polish, the faint notes of aging books), that scent becomes neurologically woven into the memory of where particular information was learned. Studies have shown that students tested on material in the same olfactory context where they learned it show improved recall. This is not because the smell is inherently mnemonic; it is because the smell was encoded as part of the context in which the learning occurred.

Architects have long understood the importance of *visual* context cues—materials, light, geometry—in creating memorable places. But the olfactory context has been entirely ignored, treated as something to be masked or removed rather than designed and deployed. This is a profound error. The olfactory context is not incidental to spatial memory; it is a core constituent of how the brain encodes and retrieves memories of place.

**Proposed experiments:**
- *IV*: Olfactory context match during learning and testing (matched scent, mismatched scent, no scent at learning, no scent at test); *DV*: free recall + recognition of information learned in the space; *Prediction*: matched olfactory context at encoding and retrieval produces maximum recall; olfactory context mismatch produces retrieval interference (similar to context-dependent memory effects).
- *IV*: Olfactory context novelty (familiar odor in familiar space, novel odor in familiar space, familiar odor in novel space); *DV*: hippocampal theta-gamma coupling (iEEG) and behavioral confidence in spatial navigation; *Prediction*: odor-space mismatch triggers hippocampal replay and reconfiguration; confidence drops until new odor-context binding is learned.
- *IV*: Piriform cortex response to odor in isolated context vs. learned spatial context (single-unit recording or fMRI); *DV*: neuronal gain, latency, population coherence; *Prediction*: piriform neurons show enhanced response and earlier engagement when odor is presented in its learned spatial context.

**Recommended specialists**: Brent Vogt (University of Iowa, piriform cortex anatomy and function), Donald Wilson (NYU, olfactory learning), Laura Shanahan (Yale, olfactory-spatial memory in naturalistic settings).

### Charles Spence: Multisensory Integration and Crossmodal Correspondences

The brain does not experience vision, hearing, smell, and touch as separate sensory streams. It binds them into a unified percept of space and place. This multisensory binding is not a passive concatenation; it is an active, integrative process governed by principles of crossmodal correspondence and temporal-spatial congruence.

Crossmodal correspondences are systematic associations between features of different sensory modalities. People consistently judge high-pitched sounds as "bright" and low-pitched sounds as "dark." They associate sharp tastes with sharp visual angles and smooth tastes with smooth curves. They link warmth with red and coldness with blue. These correspondences are not learned through instruction; they emerge early in development and appear across cultures. They reflect deep computational principles by which the multisensory brain binds disparate signals into coherent percepts.

What do crossmodal correspondences tell us about olfaction in architecture? They suggest that scents have implicit associations with visual, acoustic, and tactile properties. A "sharp," citrus odorant will be neurobiologically congruent with high-frequency acoustics, bright visual colors, and metallic or angular textures. A "warm," woody odorant will correspond to low-frequency sounds, warm visual colors, and soft, rounded forms. When these correspondences are honored in architectural design—when the olfactory character of a space aligns with its visual, acoustic, and tactile properties—multisensory integration is facilitated, and the resulting perception is *superadditive*. That is, the whole is neurobiologically greater than the sum of the parts.

The neural substrate of this superadditivity is found in multisensory neurons in the superior colliculus, posterior parietal cortex, and superior temporal sulcus. These neurons respond maximally when their preferred stimuli are presented in aligned, congruent ways. A neuron responding to congruent audio-visual motion will show a response to bimodal presentation that exceeds the sum of its unimodal responses. Incongruent or temporally misaligned stimuli produce subadditive responses—the multisensory response is *smaller* than the maximum unimodal response.

This principle extends to olfactory-visual-acoustic-tactile integration. An architectural space that is visually calm, acoustically quiet, thermally stable, and scented with soothing lavender will produce superadditive activation in multisensory integration regions. The perceived atmosphere—the felt quality of the space—will be neurobiologically richer and more salient than any unimodal component alone. Conversely, a space with incongruent properties—visually chaotic but scented with calming lavender, acoustically aggressive but thermally pleasant—will produce multisensory conflict, lowered salience, and potential behavioral stress.

The "atmosphere" or "Stimmung" that architects and philosophers speak of is, neurobiologically, a multisensory gestalt. It is the binding of visual form, acoustic character, thermal properties, haptic sensation, and olfactory signature into a holistic spatial percept. Without a theoretical framework for multisensory integration, architectural practice has been implicitly visual-centric, with other senses treated as supplements. Reframing atmospheric experience as multisensory integration opens design to previously invisible principles of coherence and impact.

One critical principle is **crossmodal enhancement vs. crossmodal facilitation**. In enhancement, one modality amplifies perception in another (e.g., visual motion enhances auditory motion perception). In facilitation, one modality makes another more perceptually accessible or salient without amplifying its intensity. Olfaction often operates via facilitation rather than enhancement. The smell of coffee does not make the visual appearance of a coffee shop *brighter* or more intense; rather, it makes the space more *recognizable*, more *contextualized*, more *semantically coherent*. This is a form of integration that affects meaning and memory more than sensory intensity.

**Proposed experiments:**
- *IV*: Crossmodal congruence (congruent odor-visual-acoustic triplet vs. systematically incongruent combinations); *DV*: multisensory evoked responses in posterior parietal cortex (fMRI), behavioral spatial comfort ratings, dwell time in space; *Prediction*: congruent triplets produce superadditive activation and elevated comfort; incongruent combinations produce subadditive activation and faster exit.
- *IV*: Crossmodal facilitation (olfactory priming before visual recognition task in architectural space); *DV*: recognition latency, confidence, fMRI activation in perirhinal cortex (visual semantics); *Prediction*: olfactory cues congruent with space identity facilitate visual recognition and reduce latency; incongruent cues impair recognition.
- *IV*: Thermal-olfactory-visual congruence (warm color + warm odorant + elevated ambient temperature vs. mismatched combinations); *DV*: anterior insula activation (fMRI), thermal comfort perception, thermoregulatory behavior; *Prediction*: congruent triplets enhance perceived thermal comfort; mismatches produce discomfort and thermoregulatory stress responses.

**Recommended specialists**: Gemma Calvert (University of Warwick, multisensory integration), Barry Stein (Wake Forest, superior colliculus multisensory neurons), Betina Ip (University of Essex, olfactory-visual integration).

### Björn Rasch: Targeted Memory Reactivation and Sleep-Dependent Consolidation

Sleep is the consolidation bottleneck for declarative memory. During sleep, particularly in slow-wave sleep and REM, the hippocampus and cortex engage in a process called "systems consolidation," whereby memories initially dependent on the hippocampus are gradually integrated into cortical long-term storage. This process is not passive replay; the brain is actively reorganizing memory traces, extracting patterns, and integrating new information with existing knowledge.

A remarkable discovery from my laboratory and others is that **targeted memory reactivation (TMR)** during sleep can selectively enhance consolidation of specific memories. If an odor is presented during learning (e.g., while studying information in a spatial context), and that same odor is presented during sleep, the presence of the odor cue during sleep enhances consolidation of the memory associated with that odor. This is not a small effect; TMR can produce 20-40% improvements in memory retention compared to sleep without the cue.

The mechanism is elegant. During sleep, the reactivation of a learned odor trace by olfactory bulb input triggers reactivation of the associated memory trace in the hippocampus. This reactivation is coordinated with cortical slow-wave oscillations, particularly the K-complex (a characteristic negative deflection in slow-wave sleep). The timing of the odor-triggered reactivation relative to slow-wave oscillations determines whether consolidation is enhanced. When the olfactory cue-triggered hippocampal replay coincides with the up-state of a slow-wave cycle, memory consolidation is accelerated.

For architectural design and memory, this opens a previously impossible possibility: **using olfactory cues during sleep to enhance consolidation of spatial memories formed in architectural spaces**. Imagine a student learning in a library infused with a distinctive scent. If that scent were presented during sleep on the night after learning, the memory for information learned in that spatial context would be consolidated more robustly. The spatial map of the library—its layout, its acoustic character, its visual features—would be integrated more deeply into long-term cortical memory.

This is not science fiction; it is straightforward application of established neurobiology. Hotels, hospitals, educational institutions, and residential spaces could be designed with specific olfactory signatures, and those signatures could be deployed during sleep (via personal diffusers, pillow sprays, or environmental systems in bedrooms) to enhance memory consolidation of spatial experiences.

Moreover, olfactory cues during sleep may trigger targeted emotional consolidation. The amygdala, which receives direct olfactory input, is highly engaged during sleep, particularly REM sleep. An odor associated with emotional experiences in an architectural space—fear in a medical setting, comfort in a home, motivation in a workspace—can cue emotional memory consolidation during sleep. This could enhance the emotional "imprinting" of a space, making its affective character more stable and persistent in memory.

There is also a potential application to therapeutic architecture. Patients with trauma or anxiety associated with particular environments could be given post-traumatic therapy in reimagined versions of those spaces, infused with new olfactory signatures. Presenting those new olfactory cues during sleep could accelerate extinction learning and emotional reconsolidation, potentially more effectively than traditional exposure therapy.

**Proposed experiments:**
- *IV*: Olfactory TMR during sleep (cue presented during SWS window vs. cue presented during non-learning window vs. no cue); *DV*: memory for spatial layout + semantic information learned in scented space; EEG markers of sleep slow-wave activity; *Prediction*: TMR during SWS windows enhances both spatial and semantic memory; effect is largest when EEG shows K-complex aligned with odor presentation.
- *IV*: Emotional memory consolidation (space + emotionally neutral task + positive olfactory cue; vs. space + emotional task + congruent/incongruent olfactory cue); *DV*: amygdala fMRI during cued recall; implicit affective bias toward space; *Prediction*: olfactory cues during sleep enhance emotional memory consolidation; congruent cues produce strongest effect.
- *IV*: Spatial navigation after TMR-enhanced consolidation vs. standard learning; *DV*: navigation efficiency (path length, errors), confidence in allocentric orientation, hippocampal theta coherence during recall; *Prediction*: TMR-enhanced learning produces faster, more confident, more efficient spatial navigation; hippocampal theta shows greater coherence and spectral power.

**Recommended specialists**: Thore Bergmann (Max Planck Institute, sleep and memory consolidation), Penelope Lewis (University of Manchester, memory consolidation mechanisms), Jan Born (University of Tübingen, sleep-dependent learning).

### Diane Ackerman: Environmental Olfaction and the Phenomenology of Atmosphere

The "atmosphere" of a place—what the German architectural tradition calls "Stimmung"—is the felt quality that emerges from the integration of all sensory properties of an environment. It is what you feel when you step into a grand cathedral, a intimate café, a sterile hospital corridor, or a forest after rain. It is not a property of any single sense; it is an emergent property of multisensory integration.

Scent plays a disproportionate role in atmospheric perception, despite being nearly invisible in architectural discourse. This is partly because scent operates partly unconsciously, and partly because it is difficult to control and specify in building codes and design guidelines. But the neurobiological reality is that olfaction shapes atmosphere profoundly.

Consider the atmosphere of a hospital. The dominant sensory features are often visual (bright, institutional) and acoustic (beeping, footsteps, announcements). But the olfactory environment—the characteristic chemical signature of hospitals, a blend of disinfectants, medicines, bodily fluids, and decay—profoundly shapes the affective experience. Patients report that the smell of a hospital triggers anxiety and discomfort before conscious recognition. This is the direct amygdala response to olfactory input. The smell does not merely accompany the stressful visual and acoustic environment; it *amplifies* it through multisensory integration.

Conversely, the atmosphere of a spa or wellness retreat is orchestrated through multisensory congruence. Soft, warm visual colors; low-frequency acoustic environment (or nature sounds); warm, thermally comfortable temperature; and soft, natural olfactory elements (eucalyptus, lavender, cedar). Each modality independently contributes to a sense of calm and safety, but their integration creates an atmosphere that exceeds the sum of parts.

The thermal properties of a space are particularly important in atmosphere, and they interact intimately with olfaction. Temperature is perceived through thermoreceptors in skin, but also through the trigeminal nerve, which detects warming or cooling sensations in the nasal passages when breathing volatile compounds. The smell of peppermint, for instance, activates the trigeminal system, creating a perception of coolness. Warm, spicy scents (cinnamon, cumin) activate warming sensations. The interaction between thermal microclimate and olfactory-trigeminal properties shapes the perceived "warmth" or "coolness" of a space in ways that pure temperature control cannot.

Material haptics—the tactile experience of touching architectural surfaces—are another often-overlooked component of atmosphere. Wood, concrete, glass, and fabric produce different somatosensory responses. Wood's lower thermal conductivity means it feels warmer to the touch; concrete's high conductivity makes it feel cold, even at room temperature. These haptic properties interact with olfaction: the smell of wood is neurobiologically congruent with the haptic warmth of wood; the industrial smell of concrete is congruent with its tactile coldness.

What CNfA has missed is that atmosphere is not a philosophical or aesthetic category—it is a neuroscientific phenomenon. It is measurable via multisensory integration fMRI, via amygdala activation, via hippocampal spatial encoding. An atmospheric space is one in which multisensory properties align to produce maximal superadditive integration and coherent hippocampal-amygdala-cortical memory encoding. The job of architectural design is to create sensory alignment in service of atmosphere.

The practical implication is that architects must expand their palette. No longer is it sufficient to specify visual form, materials, and acoustics. The design brief must include olfactory properties: What scents are appropriate to this space? How should they vary with season, time of day, occupancy? How should they interact with thermal and haptic properties? What olfactory cues should be present during learning/occupancy to enhance subsequent memory consolidation?

**Proposed experiments:**
- *IV*: Architectural atmosphere manipulation (congruent multisensory properties vs. systematically incongruent recombinations); *DV*: multisensory integration in posterior parietal and anterior insula cortex (fMRI), amygdala activation, subjective atmosphere ratings, dwell time, behavioral comfort; *Prediction*: congruent atmospheres produce superadditive neural activation, elevated comfort ratings, and longer dwell times; incongruent recombinations produce discomfort and rapid egress.
- *IV*: Material-haptic-olfactory congruence (wood with woodsmoke, concrete with industrial scent, etc. vs. incongruent pairings); *DV*: somatosensory cortex activation to material touch, insula activation to thermal properties, piriform activation to scent, behavioral material preference; *Prediction*: congruent triplets produce superadditive somatosensory-insula-piriform integration; behavioral preference aligns with integration strength.
- *IV*: Temporal dynamics of atmospheric perception (initial impression vs. 15 min vs. 1 hour occupancy); *DV*: amygdala and hippocampal fMRI, olfactory adaptation (electrophysiology), behavioral state changes (stress, comfort, arousal); *Prediction*: early amygdala response dominates initial atmosphere perception; hippocampal consolidation dominates with sustained occupancy; olfactory adaptation narrows olfactory contribution but multisensory integration maintains atmospheric character.

**Recommended specialists**: Anton Ehrenzweig (posthumous, but see works on aesthetic depth), James Turrell (visual perception and light, applicable to multisensory), Alvar Aalto (environmental design principles, though not a neuroscientist, his work exemplifies atmospheric principle).

## Cross-Panelist Synthesis

### The Olfactory-Hippocampal Direct Pathway and Architectural Memory

The consensus among panelists is that olfaction's direct access to the hippocampus and amygdala is not a minor technical detail—it is the fundamental reason olfaction is the *most powerful* sense for spatial memory encoding. Yeshurun's documentation of olfactory-limbic speed and strength, combined with Gottfried's work on context-dependent piriform coding and Rasch's TMR paradigm, points to a mechanism by which architectural scent becomes *neurologically inscribed* into place memory with exceptional durability.

The hippocampus maintains allocentric spatial maps—neuronal representations of the layout of space independent of the observer's position. These maps are constructed from visual input (place cells, grid cells), but they are rapidly enriched by olfactory input. An odor associated with a particular location becomes a permanent feature of the place cell's firing pattern; the place cell responds maximally when the animal is in that location *and* the associated odor is present. Over learning, the olfactory component becomes so strongly bound to the spatial representation that the odor alone can reactivate the place cell.

For architectural memory, this means that a space with a distinctive, designed olfactory signature will be encoded with higher fidelity and greater durability than a space without olfactory differentiation. This is not metaphorical; it is a measurable phenomenon in hippocampal neuronal recordings. A hospital corridor with a distinctive green-floral scent will generate a richer, more stable hippocampal spatial representation than an unscented corridor. Patients will navigate that space more efficiently, remember its layout more accurately, and experience it as more "memorable" or "distinctive"—because neurobiologically, it is.

The implication for architectural practice is profound: **olfactory design should be considered a tier-1 element of spatial memory architecture, equivalent to geometric form and material**. The designers of educational buildings, healthcare facilities, and public spaces should be asking: What olfactory signature will enhance the spatial memory encoding of this place? How will that signature interact with the visual, acoustic, and thermal environment? How can that signature be deployed during sleep to enhance consolidation?

### Targeted Memory Reactivation and Sleep-Dependent Enhancement

Rasch's TMR paradigm introduces a novel capability: the possibility of enhancing memory consolidation of spatial experiences *after occupancy*, during sleep. This breaks the traditional model where architectural experience is fixed and immutable once the space is occupied.

Imagine a hospital patient hospitalized for a week. The patient spends 24 hours in a hospital room, during which they experience spatial learning, emotional experiences, and medical procedures. The architectural environment—its visual form, its acoustic properties, its olfactory character—is encoded into the patient's hippocampus. That encoding determines how the patient navigates the hospital, how they feel emotionally in different zones, how they remember the experience.

Now, if the hospital room is infused with a distinctive scent (e.g., lavender or bergamot), and that scent is presented during the patient's sleep via a bedside diffuser, the TMR paradigm predicts that memory consolidation of the spatial experience will be enhanced. The patient will remember the hospital layout more accurately, the sensory character of the space more vividly, and the affective tone more durably.

This opens therapeutic applications: a hospital could deliberately design its olfactory environment to support consolidation of positive, healing memories. A patient who undergoes a successful treatment could have that success neurobiologically "imprinted" into spatial memory through post-treatment olfactory cuing during sleep. Conversely, in trauma therapy, a traumatic space could be reimagined with new olfactory properties, and those properties presented during sleep to facilitate emotional reconsolidation and trauma processing.

This requires coordination between architectural design and sleep neuroscience—unprecedented in current practice. But the neuroscience is unambiguous: **post-occupancy olfactory cuing during sleep can enhance architectural memory consolidation by 20-40%**.

### Crossmodal Congruence and the "Atmosphere" as Neurobiological Phenomenon

Spence's work on crossmodal correspondences combined with Ackerman's phenomenology of atmosphere reveals that the felt quality of an architectural space is a direct reflection of multisensory integration strength. Spaces that are neurobiologically congruent—where visual, acoustic, thermal, haptic, and olfactory properties align—produce superadditive multisensory responses and are experienced as more coherent, more comfortable, and more memorable.

The "atmosphere" or "Stimmung" is not an aesthetic judgment or subjective preference; it is a measurable property of multisensory integration. A therapeutically designed space (e.g., a mental health clinic) should have:

- **Visual properties**: soft colors, rounded forms, non-institutional lighting
- **Acoustic properties**: low ambient noise, natural or soft music if any, sound absorption that prevents harsh reflections
- **Thermal properties**: stable, warm (but not hot) ambient temperature
- **Olfactory properties**: soft, natural, slightly pleasant scents (lavender, cedarwood, subtle florals)
- **Haptic properties**: soft textures on surfaces (wood, fabric), materials with low thermal conductivity that feel warm to the touch

Each of these properties independently contributes to a sense of safety and calm. But critically, their multisensory *alignment*—the fact that they are all coordinating in the same direction toward warmth, softness, and comfort—produces a superadditive neural response that no single modality alone could achieve.

Conversely, institutional spaces often exhibit multisensory *misalignment*:

- **Visual properties**: bright, institutional, hard-edged (visual cool)
- **Acoustic properties**: high ambient noise, hard reflections (acoustic harshness)
- **Thermal properties**: variable, sometimes cold (thermal discomfort)
- **Olfactory properties**: disinfectant, chemical, slightly aversive
- **Haptic properties**: cold materials (metal, tile), hard textures

Each property independently activates aversion networks in the amygdala and anterior insula. The multisensory *alignment* toward aversion and discomfort produces a subadditive suppression of comfort perception—the space is experienced as more hostile and stressful than any single sensory component alone would suggest.

The implication for architectural practice is that **atmosphere should be a designed, measurable outcome**, not an incidental byproduct. The brief should specify target multisensory integration properties. The design should be evaluated via multisensory integration metrics (fMRI-based superadditivity indices, subjective atmosphere scales) before construction.

### Thermal-Olfactory-Visual Integration and Perceived Comfort

A particular domain of multisensory integration that deserves emphasis is the thermal-olfactory axis. Temperature is perceived through peripheral thermoreceptors (TRPV1, TRPV3 for warmth; TRPA1 for coolness) and through the trigeminal nerve, which detects temperature changes in nasal passages and mucous membranes when volatile compounds are inhaled.

Many volatile compounds activate trigeminal receptors. Peppermint and eucalyptus create a cooling sensation; cinnamon and capsaicin create a warming sensation. These are not learned associations; they are direct trigeminal activation. When a space is thermally warm but scented with cooling odorants (e.g., peppermint), the olfactory-trigeminal signal conflicts with the thermal signal, producing a kind of multisensory confusion. Conversely, when a space is thermally cool but scented with warming odorants (cinnamon, spice), the olfactory-trigeminal signal enhances the comfort perception.

This opens a novel design strategy: **thermal comfort can be modulated via olfactory-trigeminal properties without changing ambient temperature**. A space that is thermally neutral but scented with warming spices will be perceived as warmer and more comfortable than an unscented space. A space that is thermally warm but scented with cooling eucalyptus will be perceived as more comfortable in summer. This is not mere suggestion; it is direct trigeminal-thermal integration in the anterior insula and posterior cingulate cortex.

The practical application is in energy efficiency and adaptive comfort. Rather than running heating and cooling to achieve the perceived comfort of a space, designers could modulate olfactory-trigeminal properties to achieve comfort at lower energy cost. A datacenter or industrial space could be maintained at lower temperature but scented with warming compounds, reducing thermal comfort deficits. A naturally warm space (e.g., a greenhouse or sunlit courtyard) could be scented with cooling odorants to extend perceived thermal comfort.

### Olfactory Adaptation and Dynamic Scent Design

A critical insight from Yeshurun's work is that olfactory adaptation—the rapid habituation to continuous odorants—is not a bug but a feature. The olfactory system evolved to detect *change*, not stasis. An architectural space with a static, unchanging olfactory profile will lose its olfactory character within 5-10 minutes of occupancy.

This suggests that architectural olfaction requires **dynamic scent delivery**. Rather than a single diffuser maintaining a constant concentration of a single odorant, the design should incorporate:

- **Temporal variation**: Scent concentration rises and falls over hours, mimicking natural diurnal olfactory variation
- **Compositional complexity**: Multiple odorant notes that blend and shift, preventing adaptation to individual components
- **Contextual variation**: Different scents for different times of day, different seasons, different activities
- **Novelty introduction**: Periodic introduction of related but novel odorants to reset adaptation

A library might be scented with a base note of aged paper and wood, with secondary notes of leather and subtle florals. Over the course of a day, the balance shifts—more leather in morning, more floral in afternoon, fading in evening. Every few weeks, a new note is introduced (perhaps tobacco leaf or exotic spice), just distinct enough to reset olfactory attention without disorienting regular users.

This dynamic approach honors the olfactory system's evolutionary purpose while maintaining the architectural benefits of olfactory distinctiveness and memory enhancement.

### New Mechanisms for K-ATLAS: Olfactory and Multisensory Tiers

The current K-ATLAS framework treats Multisensory Integration as Tier-1 but implementation is thin. Based on panel testimony, we recommend substantial expansion:

**Tier-1: Olfactory Foundations**
- Olfactory anatomy (bulb → piriform → amygdala/hippocampus direct pathway)
- Odor perception and adaptation dynamics
- Olfactory-limbic binding and emotional tagging
- Olfactory context-dependent memory

**Tier-2: Multisensory Integration Mechanisms**
- Crossmodal correspondences (pitch-brightness, temperature-taste, etc.)
- Multisensory enhancement vs. facilitation
- Superadditive neural integration in multisensory cortex
- Temporal-spatial binding window

**Tier-3: Architectural Applications**
- Atmosphere design (Stimmung as multisensory gestalt)
- Olfactory-spatial memory encoding

## New Stimulus Properties for Measurement

### Olfactory Stimulus Dimensions

Odorants vary along multiple perceptual dimensions beyond simple "pleasant/unpleasant":

| Dimension | Scale | Relevance to Architecture |
|-----------|-------|--------------------------|
| **Intensity** | Low (threshold) to High (strong) | Controls initial amygdala engagement and adaptation rate |
| **Pleasantness** | Aversive (-5) to Pleasant (+5) | Predicts affective response and dwell time |
| **Familiarity** | Novel to Extremely familiar | Shapes memory retrieval and contextual binding |
| **Edibility** | Inedible to Highly edible-seeming | Engages olfactory-gustatory integration; affects appetite centers |
| **Source identifiability** | Unidentifiable to Highly identifiable (e.g., "coffee") | Semantic binding and conceptual integration |
| **Trigeminal potency** | Non-irritating to Strongly irritating/warming/cooling | Thermal-olfactory integration and comfort perception |
| **Volatility** | Low (slow release) to High (rapid volatilization) | Controls adaptation dynamics and scent persistence |

### Multisensory Congruence Indices

Multisensory congruence can be quantified via:

- **Cross-modal correspondence strength**: Degree to which olfactory, visual, acoustic, and thermal properties align along perceptual dimensions (e.g., all "warm" vs. mixed "warm" and "cool")
- **Superadditivity index**: fMRI-based measure of multisensory response (bimodal response divided by sum of unimodal responses; >1.0 indicates superadditivity)
- **Atmospheric coherence**: Subjective rating of perceived unity and integration of sensory properties (1-7 scale)

### Haptic and Material Properties

Material properties relevant to architectural neuroscience:

| Property | Measurement | Relevance |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Thermal conductivity** | W/(m·K) | Determines perceived warmth/coldness on contact; activates anterior insula |
| **Surface roughness** | Micrometers | Affects somatosensory cortex response; influences perceived quality and luxury |
| **Compliance (elasticity)** | Compression resistance | Engages proprioceptive feedback; influences comfort and stability perception |
| **Surface temperature** | °C at contact | Overrides ambient temperature perception via peripheral thermoreceptors |
| **Texture complexity** | Spatial frequency analysis | Shapes somatosensory cortex response patterns |

## Recommended Future Panels

1. **Pediatric Olfactory Development and Architectural Memory Formation in Children**
   - Focus: How do children's olfactory systems develop? When do odor-evoked memories become autobiographical and emotionally potent? How can educational and residential spaces be designed to support healthy olfactory-spatial memory development?
   - Panelists: Kristien Gevers (developmental olfaction), Ulrike Kaisinger (infant memory development)

2. **Olfactory Pathology and Architectural Implications: Anosmia, Hyposmia, and Neurodegeneration**
   - Focus: Alzheimer's disease involves early olfactory loss. How do spaces need to be adapted for olfactorily impaired occupants? What role can olfactory rehabilitation play?
   - Panelists: Thomas Doty (olfactory loss and neurodegenerative disease), Marsel Mesulam (olfactory dysfunction in Alzheimer's)

3. **Synesthesia, Cross-Modal Perception Variations, and Universal Architectural Design**
   - Focus: Some people experience synesthetic cross-activation (seeing colors when smelling odors). How should universal design principles account for individual variation in multisensory integration?
   - Panelists: Vilayanur Ramachandran (synesthesia), Jamie Ward (grapheme-color synesthesia and mechanism)

4. **Chemosensory Neuropathways in Wayfinding, Spatial Anxiety, and Agoraphobia**
   - Focus: Does olfactory cuing improve wayfinding in complex spaces? Can olfactory design reduce anxiety in large, open spaces? Mechanisms underlying olfactory influence on spatial anxiety.
   - Panelists: May-Britt Moser (spatial navigation systems), Tim Dalgleish (emotion and spatial cognition)

5. **Microbiome, Airborne Pathogens, and Olfactory-Immune Integration**
   - Focus: The olfactory epithelium is directly exposed to airborne pathogens and volatile organic compounds produced by skin microbiota. How does this immune-olfactory interaction shape perception of spaces as "clean," "healthy," or "infected"?
   - Panelists: Paul Eggert (olfaction and immune function), Christoph Klenk (microbial VOCs and health)
