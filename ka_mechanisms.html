Causal Mechanisms

Mechanisms are the proposed causal chains that link an environmental condition to a measured outcome. Each card specifies the condition, the intervening variables, the outcome, and the evidentiary status of every link in the chain.

Browse mechanisms

Mechanisms are indexed by the outcome they explain. A mechanism whose first causal step is well-supported but whose downstream steps are speculative shows a partial-support indicator: you can see where the chain strengthens and where it weakens.

Noise → cortisol → cognitive performance
Well-supported Open-plan offices; classrooms
Acoustic Stress

Chain: Chronic background noise > 55 dBA → sustained cortisol elevation → hippocampal-dependent memory decrement.

Evidentiary status: Steps 1–2 supported by multiple RCTs and field studies; step 3 supported but effect-size contested.

Daylight → circadian entrainment → alertness
Well-supported Office and residential
Luminous Melanopsin

Chain: Morning exposure to ≥ 250 melanopic lux → intrinsically-photosensitive retinal ganglion cell activation → suprachiasmatic nucleus phase advance → daytime alertness gains.

Ceiling height → construal level → creativity
Partial support Meyers-Levy & Zhu · 2007
Spatial Cognition

Chain: High ceiling → activation of "freedom" construct → abstract construal → creative idea generation.

Evidentiary status: Original finding replicates in some laboratory contexts and fails in others; real-world field effect is modest.

Thermal comfort → affect → prosocial behaviour
Speculative Williams & Bargh · 2008 (challenged)
Thermal Social

Chain: Warmer ambient temperature → primed "warmth" concept → prosocial judgement.

Evidentiary status: Original priming result has failed to replicate in large multi-lab efforts; listed here as a cautionary mechanism to revise or retire.

Crowding → cognitive load → error rate
Partial support Dense residential; healthcare
Crowding Attention

Chain: Perceived social density → working-memory load from inhibiting social monitoring → increased error rate on concurrent tasks.

Materiality → thermal affect → room preference
Partial support Wood vs. metal surfaces
Material Affect

Chain: Touch of warm-feeling wood surface → lowered skin-conductance response → positive affect → room preference.
